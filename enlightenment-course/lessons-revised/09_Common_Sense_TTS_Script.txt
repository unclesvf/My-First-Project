Welcome to today's history lesson. Common Sense. Thomas Paine and the Pamphlet That Ignited a Revolution.

You will hear this story from Nathaniel, a fifteen year old printer's devil in Philadelphia, January seventeen seventy-six.

Chapter One. The Mysterious Manuscript.

My name is Nathaniel, and I am fifteen years old. I work as a printer's devil — the boy who does the dirty work — at Robert Bell's printing shop in Philadelphia. And this January of 1776, I have helped print a pamphlet that is setting all of America on fire.

The manuscript arrived just after Christmas, delivered by a man with a plain face and intense eyes. His name, I learned later, was Thomas Paine — an Englishman who had arrived in America barely a year ago with nothing but a letter of introduction from Benjamin Franklin.

"This must be printed immediately," Paine told Mr. Bell. "And it must be sold cheaply — no more than two shillings. Every farmer, every shopkeeper, every common man must be able to afford it."

The pamphlet was titled Common Sense. When I first helped set the type, I thought it was simply another political essay like so many others. I was wrong. This is dynamite wrapped in paper.

Within weeks, Common Sense has sold over 100,000 copies — in a nation of only two and a half million people. It is being read aloud in taverns, churches, and town squares. It is transforming the American argument from one about taxes and rights into something far more radical: complete independence from Britain.

Chapter Two. Plain Truth for Plain People.

What makes Common Sense so powerful? I have puzzled over this as I read the pages coming off our press. Other writers have criticized Parliament and King George. Why do Paine's words strike like lightning?

The answer, I realize, is in the title itself. Paine writes in plain language that ordinary people can understand. He does not write like a lawyer or a philosopher, hedging every statement with qualifications. He writes with passion, clarity, and devastating logic.

"Society in every state is a blessing," Paine declares, "but Government, even in its best state, is but a necessary evil; in its worst state, an intolerable one."

He attacks not just King George but the very idea of monarchy. Why should one man, simply because of his birth, rule over millions? "Of more worth is one honest man to society," Paine writes, "than all the crowned ruffians that ever lived."

This is shocking. Many colonists still hope for reconciliation with Britain, still profess loyalty to the King while opposing Parliament's taxes. Paine demolishes this distinction. The problem is not bad ministers or corrupt policies — the problem is monarchy itself.

"There is something exceedingly ridiculous in the composition of monarchy," he argues. The idea that a nation's fate should depend on the accident of birth — whether the next king is wise or foolish, kind or cruel — is absurd. Hereditary monarchy is, in Paine's memorable phrase, "an insult and an imposition on posterity."

Chapter Three. The Case for Independence.

But Paine does more than attack monarchy. He makes the positive case for American independence with arguments that seem so obvious once he states them that people wonder why they did not see it before.

Look at a map, Paine urges. America is a vast continent. Britain is a small island thousands of miles away. "There is something absurd in supposing a continent to be perpetually governed by an island." Nature itself has decreed that America must be independent.

Consider commerce. Britain's wars drag America into conflicts with nations that would otherwise be trading partners. Independence would open American ports to the entire world. "Our plan is commerce," Paine writes, "and that, well attended to, will secure us the peace and friendship of all Europe."

Think of the future. The population of America is growing rapidly. Will future generations accept being ruled by a distant Parliament in which they have no voice? "The authority of Great Britain over this continent is a form of government which sooner or later must have an end."

And consider the present moment. Britain has already made war on the colonies. Lexington, Concord, Bunker Hill — blood has been spilled. Reconciliation is not merely unwise but impossible. "The blood of the slain, the weeping voice of nature cries, 'TIS TIME TO PART."

Chapter Four. A New Kind of Government.

Paine does not merely argue for independence — he sketches what an independent America might look like. And here, the Enlightenment philosophy that shaped Locke, Montesquieu, and Rousseau finds its most popular expression.

"Let the assemblies be annual, with a President only," Paine proposes. Representatives should be chosen by the people and accountable to them. Laws should be made by elected legislators, not decreed by kings. A written constitution should be "the political bible" of the nation, protecting the rights of all.

"But where, says some, is the King of America?" Paine asks. His answer is revolutionary: "In America THE LAW IS KING. For as in absolute governments the King is law, so in free countries the law ought to be king, and there ought to be no other."

This is the Enlightenment dream made concrete. Not rule by a person but rule by law. Not the arbitrary will of a monarch but the reasoned consent of the governed. Not tradition and hereditary privilege but principles of justice applied equally to all.

Paine calls for religious tolerance and freedom of conscience. He envisions a nation that would be "an asylum for mankind" — a refuge for the oppressed of all nations. "O! receive the fugitive," he urges, "and prepare in time an asylum for mankind."

Chapter Five. The Flame That Spreads.

I watch the effects of Common Sense ripple outward from our printing shop like waves from a stone cast into water.

Men who were uncertain become resolute. Tavern debates that went in circles suddenly find direction. Ministers read passages from their pulpits. Militia officers read it to their troops. George Washington himself writes that Common Sense is "working a powerful change in the minds of many men."

By spring, colony after colony is instructing its delegates to vote for independence. The momentum becomes unstoppable. And in July, Thomas Jefferson sits down to write a declaration that will make official what Paine has already accomplished in the hearts and minds of the people.

Thomas Paine receives no great reward for his service. He assigns the copyright to the states and takes no royalties. He does this for the cause, not for himself.

But nothing quite matches the impact of Common Sense. In that one pamphlet, Paine proves that ideas matter, that words can change history, and that the Enlightenment vision of government by consent is not merely philosophy but common sense.

As I sweep the ink and debris from our shop floor each night, I know I have been part of something extraordinary. A man with nothing but a pen has helped convince a nation to declare itself free. That is the power of ideas clearly expressed and fearlessly spoken.
