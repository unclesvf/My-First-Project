## LESSON 27: Indian Removal - The Trail of Tears

**Current:** 2 chapters, ~120 words total
**Expanded:** 5 chapters, ~1,100 words total

### Expanded Story

**Narrator:** Sarah, age 14, Cherokee girl, 1838

#### Chapter 1: Our Cherokee Life

My name is Sarah, and I am fourteen years old. I am Cherokee—Tsalagi, we call ourselves. I live with my parents, my grandmother, and my baby sister in the rolling hills of northern Georgia, land our people have called home for longer than anyone can remember.

Our town doesn't look like what whites imagine when they think of Indians. We have wooden houses with stone chimneys, not wigwams. Our family farms corn, beans, and squash. Father raises cattle and chickens. Mother weaves beautiful baskets and tends her vegetable garden. My brother attends the mission school where he's learning to read and write in both Cherokee and English.

Thanks to Sequoyah's brilliant syllabary, our people have a written language. We publish a bilingual newspaper, the Cherokee Phoenix. We have our own constitution, modeled on America's, establishing three branches of government. We've adopted Christianity—our family attends church every Sunday. We thought if we became "civilized" by white standards, if we proved ourselves worthy, we could keep our land.

I remember the day Father brought home our first copy of the Cherokee Phoenix. He read aloud about how we were becoming a modern nation. Grandmother, who still remembers the old ways, shook her head. "The whites will never see us as equals," she said. "They want our land. They will take it." Father assured her times had changed. We were wrong. She was right.

#### Chapter 2: The Indian Removal Act

Everything changed in 1830 when President Andrew Jackson pushed the Indian Removal Act through Congress. The law authorized removing all eastern tribes to "Indian Territory"—a barren land beyond the Mississippi River that whites didn't want. Jackson claimed this was for our protection, that we couldn't survive surrounded by white settlement. But everyone knew the truth: whites wanted our land.

Gold had been discovered in Georgia in 1829. Suddenly our rich farmland and forests became even more valuable. Georgia passed laws stripping away our rights, declaring Cherokee law null and void. White prospectors and settlers moved onto our land while we still lived there, waiting like vultures for us to leave.

My father and Chief John Ross fought back the only way they could—through American courts. In 1832, the Supreme Court ruled in Worcester v. Georgia that Georgia had no authority over Cherokee territory, that we had the right to remain. We celebrated. We'd won! The highest court in America had sided with us.

But President Jackson refused to enforce the ruling. "John Marshall has made his decision," Jackson reportedly said, "now let him enforce it." I didn't understand at first. Father explained that laws only matter if those with power choose to follow them. Jackson controlled the army. The Supreme Court had no soldiers. Our legal victory meant nothing.

I watched Father's hope die. He'd believed in American justice, in the rule of law. Now he understood that might makes right, that we'd never be treated as equals no matter how "civilized" we became. Mother held him as he wept. It was the first time I'd seen my father cry.

#### Chapter 3: Soldiers Come

For six years, we resisted. We refused to sign treaties selling our land. We petitioned Congress. We published articles in our newspaper. We prayed. But in May 1838, federal troops arrived under General Winfield Scott. Seven thousand soldiers came to force 16,000 Cherokee from our homes.

I was working in our garden when I saw them—a line of blue-coated soldiers with rifles marching up the road. Mother grabbed my sister and ran into the house. Father stood in the doorway, arms crossed. "This is our land," he said quietly. "We're not leaving."

The sergeant, a young man with a hard face, pointed his rifle at Father's chest. "You have two hours to gather what you can carry. Anyone who resists will be shot." His voice shook slightly. I wondered if he was afraid, or ashamed, or both.

We scrambled to pack. What do you take when you can only carry what fits in your arms? Mother grabbed blankets, a pot, dried corn. Father took his farming tools—useless where we were going, but he couldn't leave them. Grandmother took her medicine bundle and the Bible. I held my little sister, only two years old, too young to understand why everyone was crying.

Outside, I saw our neighbors being herded into the road like cattle. Old Mr. Ridge, who couldn't walk well, was dragged from his house. Mrs. Swimmer clutched her children, her face blank with shock. The soldiers drove us toward the nearest stockade—a crude fort where they penned us like animals while they decided when to march us west.

As we stumbled down the road, I looked back at our house. White families were already moving in, claiming what we'd built. They were picking our vegetables, sitting on our porch, sleeping in our beds. Everything we'd worked for, gone. Just like Grandmother predicted.

#### Chapter 4: The Trail Where We Cried

They kept us in the stockade for weeks—overcrowded, with inadequate food and no sanitation. Disease spread: measles, whooping cough, dysentery. People died before the march even began. The soldiers buried them in unmarked graves outside the stockade walls.

Finally, in late autumn, they started marching us west. One thousand miles to Oklahoma—Indian Territory, they called it. We walked in groups of about a thousand people, guarded by soldiers. Most of us walked; only the very sick rode in wagons. The daily ration was meager: some flour, cornmeal, a bit of salt pork. Never enough.

It was the worst winter in memory. Snow came early and stayed late. We had inadequate clothing and blankets. At night, we huddled together for warmth, but still people froze. My grandmother was strong, but even she couldn't survive the cold and hunger. One morning she simply didn't wake up. We buried her beside the trail, marking the spot with stones. No time for proper burial rites, no time to mourn properly. We had to keep moving.

My baby sister lasted another week. She caught fever and wasted away in Mother's arms. Mother held her corpse for a whole day, refusing to let go, until Father gently pried the tiny body from her grasp. We buried her in Missouri, far from home. Mother hasn't spoken since.

Every day, more people died. The very old and very young went first, but even strong men and women fell. We left a trail of graves behind us—4,000 graves by the time we reached Oklahoma. We call this journey Nunna daul Tsunyi—"The Trail Where We Cried." The whites call it the Trail of Tears. Both names capture the agony, but neither can express the true horror of it.

#### Chapter 5: Oklahoma and After

We reached Oklahoma in March 1839, nearly a year after soldiers came to Georgia. Of our original group of a thousand, fewer than 600 survived. Other groups suffered similar losses. Of the 16,000 Cherokee forced west, over 4,000 died.

This land is nothing like home. It's flat, dry, desolate. The soil is poor. There are no trees like the mountains of Georgia. It's someone else's land—we're intruders here, pushing out tribes who lived here first. The cycle of dispossession continues.

We're trying to rebuild. Father constructed a crude cabin. Mother still doesn't speak, but she works mechanically—cooking, cleaning, surviving. I care for what's left of our family and try to remember Grandmother's stories, my sister's laugh, the home we lost.

The bitterest part is that America was built on ideals of liberty and justice. The Declaration of Independence says all men are created equal, endowed with rights to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness. But those words didn't include us. We had life, liberty, and happiness in Georgia. America took it all away.

I'm fourteen years old, and I've learned that a nation's ideals mean nothing if it chooses not to apply them to everyone. I've learned that "civilization" and Christianity don't protect you if you have something others want. I've learned that laws and Supreme Court rulings are meaningless without the power to enforce them.

We survived the Trail of Tears, but we're forever changed. Our culture was disrupted. Our families were torn apart. Our sacred lands are lost. The "land of the free" was built on the graves of those who lived here first. That's a truth Americans don't like to face, but it's the truth nonetheless.

Someday, I'll tell this story to my children. They need to know what was done to us, what we survived. Memory is all we have left. That, and the determination to endure.

## LESSON 38: The Civil War Begins - Bull Run to Antietam

**Current:** 2 chapters, ~140 words total
**Expanded:** 5 chapters, ~1,200 words total

### Expanded Story

**Narrator:** Thomas, age 17, Union soldier, 1862

#### Chapter 1: Joining Up

My name is Thomas, and I am seventeen years old—though I told the recruiting sergeant I was eighteen. It's July 1861, and I've just enlisted in the Union Army. The rebellion must be crushed, the Union preserved. Everyone says the war will be over by Christmas. We'll march south, thrash the rebels in one good fight, and be home for harvest.

I grew up on a farm in central Pennsylvania. My father is a blacksmith, my mother a schoolteacher. They didn't want me to enlist—I'm their only son, and they need help with the farm. But when President Lincoln called for 75,000 volunteers after Fort Sumter, I couldn't stay home while the nation was in danger.

The day I left, Mother cried and made me promise to write every week. Father shook my hand solemnly and gave me his pocket watch. "Come home safe," he said. "The farm will still be here. You're more important than any crops." I tried to look brave and manly, but my hands were shaking.

At training camp near Washington, they issued us uniforms—wool coats and trousers that were too thick for summer heat, rifles that felt impossibly heavy, leather brogans that gave us blisters. Most of the men in my regiment are farm boys like me. We don't know much about soldiering, but we're eager to prove ourselves. Our officers—many of whom don't know much more than we do—drill us in marching and loading muskets.

The camp buzzes with rumors. The rebel army is gathering near Manassas, Virginia, at a creek called Bull Run. We'll march there soon and settle this rebellion with one decisive battle. I write Mother optimistic letters about my new friends and how well training is going. I don't mention the bad food, the dysentery sweeping through camp, or how terrified I am of what's coming.

#### Chapter 2: First Bull Run—The Illusion Shatters

On July 21, 1861, we marched toward Bull Run. It was a beautiful summer morning. Civilians from Washington came out to watch—congressmen, ladies in fine dresses, families with picnic baskets. They wanted to see us crush the rebellion like it was a sporting event. Their confidence was infectious. We'd show these rebels what the Union Army could do.

The battle started mid-morning. At first, we seemed to be winning. We pushed the rebel forces back. I fired my rifle at distant figures in gray and butternut, not really aiming, just loading and firing like we'd been trained. The noise was incredible—cannon fire, musket volleys, men screaming. Smoke filled the air so thick I could barely see.

Then everything changed. A Confederate brigade under a general named Jackson held a hilltop like a stone wall—that's how he earned the nickname "Stonewall Jackson." More rebel reinforcements arrived. Suddenly we weren't pushing them back; they were pushing us. Our lines broke. Men began running.

I'll never forget the confusion and terror. Orders were shouted but no one could hear over the gunfire. Our sergeant was hit—I watched him fall, blood spreading across his chest. The man next to me dropped his rifle and ran. Then another. Then everyone was running, including me.

We fled back toward Washington in complete chaos. The civilians who'd come to watch our glorious victory were now fleeing with us, their carriages clogging the roads. Ladies screamed. Congressmen abandoned their picnic baskets. We ran all night, exhausted and humiliated.

I made it back to camp at dawn, covered in mud and shame. We'd lost nearly 500 killed, over 1,000 wounded. The rebels had beaten us badly. This war wouldn't be over by Christmas. It had barely begun. That night, I wrote Mother a very different letter. I couldn't tell her the truth—that I'd been terrified, that I'd run like a coward, that men I'd eaten breakfast with were dead. I just said the battle didn't go as planned and I was safe.

#### Chapter 3: Becoming Soldiers

After Bull Run, everything changed. The government called for more volunteers—three-year enlistments instead of the ninety days we'd signed up for. They brought in professional soldiers to train us properly. General George McClellan reorganized the Army of the Potomac, drilling us endlessly until we could maneuver like real soldiers.

The months that followed were hard but necessary. We learned discipline, how to follow orders under fire, how to load and fire in formation. We lost the naive enthusiasm of those early days. The men who'd been at Bull Run stopped talking about quick victory. We'd seen what war really meant.

I changed during those months. I was no longer the farm boy who'd enlisted thinking war was glorious. I'd seen death. I'd known terror. I'd learned that survival often depends on luck as much as courage. The Union cause was still just—the nation had to be preserved—but I understood now what that cause would cost.

I made friends in the regiment. There was Robert, a carpenter from Philadelphia with a wife and two young children. James, a schoolteacher from New York who quoted Shakespeare and wrote poetry. William, a farm boy from Ohio who reminded me of my younger self. We looked after each other, shared our rations, told stories to pass the time. In war, these friendships become more intense than any you'll know in peacetime. When death might come tomorrow, every day matters.

We spent the winter of 1861-1862 training and waiting. McClellan built a magnificent army—over 100,000 strong, well-equipped, expertly drilled. But he was cautious, always wanting more men, more supplies, more time. Lincoln grew frustrated with his delays. "If General McClellan does not want to use the army," Lincoln supposedly said, "I would like to borrow it for a time."

#### Chapter 4: Antietam—The Bloodiest Day

September 17, 1862. Antietam Creek, Maryland. I will remember this day for the rest of my life—however long that may be.

General Lee had invaded the North, and McClellan finally moved to stop him. We marched all night to reach the battlefield. At dawn, the fighting began in a cornfield. I was with the reserve units, waiting our turn. We could hear the battle—an endless roar of gunfire that sounded like the world tearing apart.

Mid-morning, we were ordered forward to attack a sunken road that the rebels were using as a defensive position. We formed lines and marched across an open field. Confederate rifles blazed from the sunken road. Men fell all around me. I saw Robert take a bullet to the chest—he was dead before he hit the ground. James lost his leg to a cannon ball. William disappeared in the smoke and I never saw him again.

Somehow I made it to the sunken road. We fired down at the rebels; they fired up at us. The fighting was desperate, hand-to-hand in places. The road filled with bodies—Confederate and Union mixed together in death. Later they'd call it "Bloody Lane" and the name fit. The carnage was beyond anything at Bull Run.

By late afternoon, the shooting finally stopped. Both armies were too exhausted to continue. I sat among the dead and dying, too numb to cry, too shocked to move. A surgeon came by checking who could be saved. I had a minor wound on my arm I didn't even remember getting. "You'll live," he said and moved on to someone who needed him more.

The next day, Lee retreated back to Virginia. Technically, we won—we held the field. But it felt like defeat. Over 23,000 men killed or wounded in a single day. I walked through the field with burial details. Bodies lay so thick in places you couldn't walk without stepping on them. The smell of blood and death was overwhelming. This wasn't war as I'd imagined it. This was industrial-scale slaughter.

#### Chapter 5: A War Transformed

Five days after Antietam, President Lincoln issued the Emancipation Proclamation. As of January 1, 1863, all slaves in rebel states would be free. The war's purpose had changed. We weren't just fighting to preserve the Union—we were fighting to end slavery.

The proclamation divided our regiment. Some men said this was what the war should have been about from the start—freeing the enslaved. Others grumbled that they'd signed up to save the Union, not to free slaves. A few even deserted rather than fight for abolition.

I thought about it long and hard. I'd grown up in Pennsylvania where slavery was illegal, but I hadn't thought much about it before the war. Now, after seeing so much death, I needed to believe we were fighting for something more than abstract political principles. The idea that we were fighting to free people from bondage gave meaning to the suffering.

Foreign powers—especially Britain and France—had been considering recognizing the Confederacy. But the Emancipation Proclamation changed everything. They wouldn't support a nation fighting to preserve slavery. The proclamation was brilliant politics and a moral statement. It transformed our war into a crusade for human freedom.

I'm nineteen now, though I feel decades older. I've been in the army for over a year. I've seen battles I can't forget and lost friends I'll always mourn. The war won't end soon—we all know that now. We might fight for years. Many of us won't survive.

But I'll keep fighting. Not for glory—I left those illusions at Bull Run. Not for adventure—there's nothing adventurous about Bloody Lane. I fight because the Union must be preserved and because slavery must end. These truths are worth the sacrifice.

I wrote Mother after Antietam—the first fully honest letter I'd sent. I told her about the horror, the death, how I'd changed. But I also told her I was proud to serve, that our cause was just, that she'd raised a son who wouldn't run from duty no matter how hard it got.

She wrote back saying she prayed for me every day, that Father was proud, that they'd hold the farm until I came home. "When this is over," she wrote, "and you've done your part to save the nation and free the enslaved, you can rest knowing you stood for what was right."

I keep that letter in my breast pocket, close to my heart, where a rebel bullet might one day find it. If I fall, at least they'll know I died believing our cause was just. That will have to be enough.
